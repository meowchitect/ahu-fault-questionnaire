AHU Fault Diagnosis Questionnaire for Bayesian Network Development

Dear Expert,

Thank you for contributing your valuable expertise to this research project. We are developing a Diagnostic Bayesian Network (DBN) to improve fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) in Air Handling Units (AHUs). Your input will be used to define and refine the probabilistic relationships between system faults and their observable symptoms. The estimated time to complete this questionnaire is approximately 20-30 minutes.

Instructions

For each of the 11 faults presented, you will be asked to provide three types of input:

  1. Fault Frequency: Judge how often the fault occurs in a typical facility.
  2. Symptom Selection: Select all symptoms that you believe could be caused by the fault.
  3. Influence & Confidence: For each symptom you selected, rate the fault's influence and your confidence in that rating.

Degree of Influence

Rate how strongly you believe the fault causes the symptom using the following linguistic scale.

  • Extremely High: The fault is a direct and almost certain cause of the symptom.
  • Very High: The fault is a very strong and likely cause.
  • High: The fault is a significant cause.
  • Medium: The fault has a moderate, noticeable influence.
  • Low: The fault has a minor or weak influence.
  • Very Low: The fault has a minimal, barely noticeable influence.
  • Extremely Low: The fault is technically related but has a negligible influence.

Confidence

Specify your confidence in your ratings.

  • A high rating indicates high confidence or greater certainty.
  • A medium rating indicates confidence between high and low.
  • A low rating indicates low confidence or greater uncertainty.

System Description & Reference

The Kropman Office Building Case Study

The Kropman office building, located in Breda, Netherlands, was selected as the case study for this research. This building, owned and operated by a building services company, serves as a typical office building in Western Europe. This building was chosen as a case study due to the convenient availability of the necessary sensor and energy meter data for model calibration. Constructed in 1993 and subsequently renovated in 2009, the building is segmented into three distinct HVAC zones: North, South, and 1.05. A central AHU manages air supply and distribution, incorporating supply and return fans, supply and return filters, a Heat Recovery Wheel (HRW), and a heating coil. Notably, cooling is provided by three separate after-coolers dedicated to each of the building's zones, rather than a central cooling coil within the AHU. Energy is supplied by a gas-fired boiler for heating demands and a chiller during the cooling season. Beyond the AHU's heating coil, radiators in each of the three zones contribute to space heating. Interior temperature regulation employs a master-slave Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) control strategy.

Key System Parameters:

  • The pre-heating sensor (Tpre) is located between the HRW and the supply fan%.
  • HRW nominal efficiency is 71%.
  • Normal CO₂ concentration should be less than 500 ppm.
  • Normal pressure setpoint is 250 Pa in winter.
  • Normal supply temperature setpoint should be less than 26°C in winter.
